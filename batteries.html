Battery Chemistry Fundamentals

Electrochemical Principles

Batteries work through electrochemical reactions that convert chemical energy to electrical energy.

Basic Electrochemical Cell:

  • Anode (Negative): Oxidation occurs, electrons are released
  • Cathode (Positive): Reduction occurs, electrons are accepted
  • Electrolyte: Ionic conductor, allows ion flow
  • Separator: Prevents direct contact between electrodes

Chemical Reactions:

  • Discharge: Li+ ions move from anode to cathode
  • Charge: Li+ ions move from cathode to anode
  • Current Flow: Electrons flow through external circuit
  • Energy Storage: Chemical potential energy in electrode materials

Battery Construction & Architecture

Battery Pack Structure

Battery Pack

Complete battery system with thermal management and electronics

Capacity: 50-150 kWh Voltage: 350-800V Weight: 300-600 kg

Module

Group of cells with shared thermal management

Capacity: 5-15 kWh Cells: 50-200 per module Voltage: 50-100V

Cell

Individual electrochemical unit

Capacity: 50-100 Ah Voltage: 3.2-4.2V Energy: 150-400 Wh

Cell Formats

Prismatic Cells

  • Shape: Rectangular prism
  • Capacity: 50-100 Ah
  • Advantages: High energy density, good thermal management
  • Used by: BMW, VW, Mercedes

Cylindrical Cells

  • Shape: Cylinder (18650, 21700, 4680)
  • Capacity: 3-25 Ah
  • Advantages: Mature technology, good thermal performance
  • Used by: Tesla, Lucid

Pouch Cells

  • Shape: Flexible pouch
  • Capacity: 20-80 Ah
  • Advantages: Lightweight, flexible design
  • Used by: GM, Hyundai, Kia

Power Density

Peak Power: 200-400 kW 0-50% SoC
Reduced Power: 100-200 kW 50-80% SoC
Limited Power: 50-100 kW 80-100% SoC

Power Limitations:

  • Internal Resistance: Increases with age and temperature
  • State of Charge: Lower power at high SoC
  • Temperature: Cold reduces power capability
  • Chemistry: LFP has higher power density than NMC

Cycle Life & Aging

Degradation Mechanisms:

  • SEI Growth: Solid electrolyte interface thickening
  • Lithium Plating: Metallic lithium deposition on anode
  • Active Material Loss: Particle cracking and isolation
  • Electrolyte Decomposition: Chemical breakdown over time

Cycle Life Factors:

  • Depth of Discharge: Shallower cycles extend life
  • Charging Rate: Fast charging accelerates aging
  • Temperature: High temperatures increase degradation
  • State of Charge: High SoC storage reduces life

Future Battery Technologies

Solid-State Batteries

Key Advantages:

  • Energy Density: 400-600 Wh/kg (2x current)
  • Safety: No flammable liquid electrolyte
  • Cycle Life: 10,000+ cycles
  • Charging Speed: 10-15 minutes to 80%

Technical Challenges:

  • Ionic Conductivity: Lower than liquid electrolytes
  • Interface Stability: Electrode-electrolyte contact
  • Manufacturing: Complex production processes
  • Cost: Currently 3-5x higher than Li-ion

Lithium-Sulfur Batteries

Potential Benefits:

  • Energy Density: 500-800 Wh/kg theoretical
  • Cost: Sulfur is abundant and cheap
  • Environmental: Less toxic materials
  • Weight: Lighter than Li-ion

Current Limitations:

  • Cycle Life: Limited to 100-500 cycles
  • Sulfur Shuttle: Polysulfide migration
  • Volume Expansion: 80% expansion during cycling
  • Low Conductivity: Sulfur is insulating
